Lesson Overview

The Bridge Between Two Worlds

CISM teaches you to govern, manage risk, run a security program, and respond to incidents — for traditional information systems. Those four domains are the foundation. But the systems your enterprise is now deploying don't behave like traditional software. They learn. They hallucinate. They can be coaxed into ignoring their instructions. They are trained on data the enterprise doesn't own and may not be able to inspect. The CISM playbook does not fully cover them — and ISACA knows it.

In August 2025, ISACA launched the Advanced in AI Security Management (AAISM) credential, available only to CISM and CISSP holders. AAISM is not a replacement — it's a bridge. It assumes you already know how to govern security; it adds what AI specifically demands. This lesson walks the bridge.

Anchoring Analogy: The Pilot's Type Rating

An airline pilot earns a base license — the ATPL. That license proves they can fly. But every new aircraft type requires a separate "type rating" because each aircraft has its own systems, its own failure modes, its own emergency procedures. The pilot doesn't unlearn how to fly; they extend their qualifications to a new platform. CISM is the ATPL. AAISM is the type rating for AI.

Learning Objectives

  1. Explain why CISM alone is insufficient for AI-enabled enterprises and where AAISM extends it.
  2. Identify the major AI governance frameworks (NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001, EU AI Act) and how they interrelate.
  3. Apply the four NIST AI RMF functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) to enterprise AI deployment.
  4. Classify AI systems under the EU AI Act's four risk tiers and identify required obligations.
  5. Differentiate AI risk categories: model risk, data risk, operational risk, ethical risk, third-party risk.
  6. Recognize shadow AI patterns and apply appropriate governance responses.
  7. Conduct vendor and supply-chain risk assessments for AI-enabled solutions.
  8. Distinguish provider, deployer, and importer responsibilities under emerging AI regulation.
  9. Develop an enterprise AI acceptable use policy that holds up under audit.

Module 1 45 min

Why CISM Alone Isn't Enough

The CISM body of knowledge was built around four enduring domains: information security governance, information risk management, information security program development, and incident management. These remain correct. They are also incomplete when the assets being governed include systems that learn from data, generate novel outputs, and can be manipulated through their inputs in ways that have no parallel in traditional software.

What CISM Covers — and Where It Stops

🏛️ CISM (Four Domains)
  • D1 Governance — strategy, policy, roles, stakeholder alignment
  • D2 Risk Management — assess, treat, monitor information security risk
  • D3 Program Development — design, implement, manage the security program
  • D4 Incident Management — prepare, detect, respond, recover, learn

Implicit assumption: Systems behave deterministically. Inputs map predictably to outputs. The asset inventory is knowable. Threats target code, not training data.

🤖 AAISM (Three Domains)
  • D1 AI Governance & Program Management (31%) — AI-specific policy, data and model lifecycle, AI incident response
  • D2 AI Risk Management (31%) — AI-specific threats, vulnerabilities, vendor and supply chain
  • D3 AI Technologies & Controls (38%) — security architecture, lifecycle controls, monitoring, ethics & trust

New assumptions: Systems behave probabilistically. Inputs can be adversarial. Training data is part of the attack surface. Models drift. Outputs can be wrong with high confidence.

Eligibility Requirement

AAISM is only available to CISM or CISSP holders. ISACA designed it as a stackable specialization, not a standalone credential. The implicit message: you cannot competently govern AI security without first understanding how to govern security in general.

For a CISM, the natural extension is AAISM. The credential explicitly assumes CISM-level expertise in governance, risk, program development, and incident management — and adds the AI-specific layer on top.

Module 2 45 min

The Governance Gap — Why "Just Add AI to the Policy" Fails

When a new technology arrives, the first instinct of mature organizations is to fold it into existing governance: "AI is just another technology — our acceptable use policy already covers it." This is wrong in ways that matter.

The Three Forces Creating the Gap

⚡

Velocity

AI capabilities are advancing on a monthly cadence. Policies written for a six-month review cycle are stale before they reach the second review.

Symptom: The AI section of your AUP cites GPT-3.5 and Bard.
🌍

Pervasiveness

AI features are arriving inside tools the enterprise already pays for — without procurement review, without an architecture decision, sometimes without a release note.

Symptom: Your Office suite, CRM, IDE, and customer support tool all gained AI features last quarter, none of them flagged.
👤

Personalization

Individual employees can adopt powerful AI tools faster than any IT review process. The "enterprise" no longer holds a monopoly on capability rollout.

Symptom: The marketing team is already shipping content from tools the CISO has never heard of.
The Failure Mode

The combination is the danger. Velocity means policy can't keep up. Pervasiveness means AI is everywhere whether you approved it or not. Personalization means employees out-pace IT. The result: AI governance done well requires entirely new mechanisms, not a paragraph appended to the existing AUP.

What Mature AI Governance Looks Like

AAISM's first supporting task says it plainly: "Collaborate on charter, roles, and responsibilities for governance and management of AI to align with business objectives." That is governance language — and it implies that AI gets its own governance structure, not a footnote in someone else's.

  • An AI Steering Committee or AI Governance Board — cross-functional (legal, privacy, security, business, ethics) with named authority and quarterly cadence at minimum.
  • An AI Use Case Register — every approved AI deployment recorded with owner, purpose, risk tier, and review date. Functions like a system inventory for AI.
  • An AI Acceptable Use Policy — distinct from the general AUP, addressing data classification rules for AI inputs, prohibited use cases, and approval pathways.
  • An AI Incident Response Annex — the standard IR plan extended to cover AI-specific incidents (hallucination-caused harm, prompt injection, data exfiltration via AI tools, deepfake-enabled fraud).
  • Defined Roles — at minimum an AI Governance Lead, AI Risk Owner, and AI Security Lead. Larger organizations add Model Owner, Data Steward for AI, and Responsible AI Officer.
Analogy: Adding a Hospital Wing

A general hospital that opens a cardiac surgery wing does not simply tell the existing staff "now you do hearts too." It hires cardiac surgeons, builds dedicated ORs, writes cardiac-specific protocols, and establishes cardiac-specific incident review. The hospital's overall governance still applies — but a new specialty requires new infrastructure. AI governance is the cardiac wing of your security program.

Practice: Spotting Governance Gaps

An employee asks: "We already have an Acceptable Use Policy that says 'use company tools responsibly.' Why do we need a separate AI policy?" Which response is BEST?

The Framework Landscape — NIST, ISO, EU, and How They Fit

The good news: you do not need to invent AI governance from scratch. Several major frameworks already exist, and a CISM-trained professional will recognize the patterns. The challenge is knowing which framework answers which question — and how they layer.

The Four Anchors

FrameworkTypeWhat It Gives YouCISM Analogue
NIST AI RMF 1.0Voluntary risk management framework (US)Risk management operating model — Govern, Map, Measure, ManageLike NIST CSF 2.0 — voluntary, function-based
ISO/IEC 42001:2023Certifiable management system standardAI Management System (AIMS) — auditable controls and processesLike ISO 27001 — certifiable management system
EU AI Act (Reg. 2024/1689)Binding regulationLegal obligations by risk tier — fines up to €35M or 7% of global turnoverLike GDPR — extraterritorial, tiered, enforced
OWASP Top 10 for LLMThreat referenceCatalog of LLM-specific vulnerabilities and mitigationsLike OWASP Top 10 for web — practitioner-driven threat list
How They Layer

Most mature programs use NIST AI RMF as the risk-management operating model inside an ISO 42001 management system, and use that combination to meet EU AI Act obligations. Layered, not alternative. OWASP sits underneath as the threat reference your controls are designed against.

The CISM-to-AI Framework Map

A CISM already knows how to position frameworks. The mental model is the same — only the names change.

If you ask this question…The framework that answers it…
"How should we organize AI risk management?"NIST AI RMF (functions, categories, subcategories)
"How do we build a certifiable AI management system?"ISO/IEC 42001
"What is legally required of us in the EU?"EU AI Act
"What threats should our controls actually defend against?"OWASP Top 10 for LLM, MITRE ATLAS
"How do we evaluate model performance and trustworthiness?"NIST AI RMF Measure function + ISO/IEC 24029 (robustness)
"What does an AI impact assessment look like?"ISO/IEC 42005, EU AI Act Article 27 (FRIA for high-risk)

The Trustworthiness Characteristics

NIST AI RMF lists seven characteristics of trustworthy AI. Memorize these — they appear across all the major frameworks under different names.

✅

Valid & Reliable

The model produces correct outputs consistently for the intended use case.

🛡️

Safe

The system does not endanger life, health, property, or the environment under foreseeable use.

🔒

Secure & Resilient

Resists adversarial attack; degrades gracefully; recoverable.

📋

Accountable & Transparent

Decisions are documented; responsibility is assigned; behavior can be explained at an appropriate level.

🔍

Explainable & Interpretable

Outputs can be traced and reasoned about by humans where consequential.

🔐

Privacy-Enhanced

Personal data is protected through data minimization, differential privacy, federated approaches where feasible.

⚖️

Fair (Bias Managed)

Harmful biases are identified, measured, and mitigated; outcomes do not systematically disadvantage protected groups.

Watch the Tradeoffs

These characteristics are not always mutually reinforcing. Explainability and accuracy can pull in opposite directions. A simpler model may be more explainable but less accurate; a state-of-the-art model may be highly accurate but opaque. Mature AI governance documents these tradeoffs explicitly rather than pretending they don't exist.

Module 4 75 min

NIST AI RMF — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage

If a CISM remembers one AI framework, it should be the NIST AI RMF 1.0. It is voluntary, technology-agnostic, sector-agnostic, and (most importantly) structurally parallel to NIST CSF 2.0 — meaning it slots into the mental models a CISM already has. The framework organizes AI risk management around four core functions.

Click each function below to see what it covers, who owns it, and where the common failure modes lie. Govern is cross-cutting — it sits above the other three and informs them all.

Analogy: The Quality Management Cycle

NIST AI RMF follows the same pattern as Deming's Plan-Do-Check-Act, with a governance layer wrapped around it. Govern sets the culture and authority. Map establishes context (what is this AI for, who does it affect, what could go wrong?). Measure turns context into evidence (what are the actual risks, in numbers?). Manage acts on the evidence and feeds findings back to Govern. It is continuous, not one-and-done.

How NIST AI RMF Maps to the CISM Domains

A CISM should be able to see the parallels immediately. The functions are not new ideas — they are familiar ideas applied to a new asset class.

NIST AI RMF FunctionCISM Domain ParallelKey Activities
Govern (cross-cutting)D1 Information Security GovernanceAI policy, AI steering committee, accountability structures, training, legal/regulatory alignment
MapD2 Information Risk Management (context establishment)Document AI system purpose, identify stakeholders, characterize impacts, categorize the system
MeasureD2 Information Risk Management (assessment) + D3 Program Development (metrics)Quantify risks, evaluate trustworthiness characteristics, set up monitoring infrastructure
ManageD2 Risk Management (treatment) + D3 Program (controls) + D4 Incident ManagementAllocate resources to treat risk, document residual risk, respond to incidents, integrate back to Govern
Bridge Insight

A CISM who understands NIST CSF 2.0 already understands 80% of NIST AI RMF. The "Govern" function was added to CSF 2.0 in 2024 precisely to align the two frameworks. The mental model is the same — the asset class is different.

The Govern Function — Why It Sits on Top

Govern is unique because it appears at the top of the diagram, not in sequence with the other three. This isn't decorative — it reflects the operational reality. Without governance, Map produces context nobody acts on, Measure produces metrics nobody reviews, and Manage produces decisions nobody owns.

  • Govern 1 — Policies, processes, procedures across the organization related to AI risks are in place, transparent, and implemented effectively
  • Govern 2 — Accountability structures are in place; teams and individuals are empowered, responsible, trained
  • Govern 3 — Workforce diversity, equity, inclusion, and accessibility processes are prioritized
  • Govern 4 — Organizational teams are committed to a culture that considers and communicates AI risk
  • Govern 5 — Processes are in place for robust engagement with relevant AI actors
  • Govern 6 — Policies and procedures are in place to address AI risks and benefits arising from third-party software and data
Common Trap

Organizations new to AI governance often try to start with Measure — "let's get metrics on our AI." This puts the cart before the horse. Without Govern and Map, you don't know which metrics matter. The functions are listed in order for a reason.

Module 5 75 min

The EU AI Act — Tiers, Obligations, and the Provider/Deployer Split

The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) is the world's first comprehensive AI law. Like GDPR, its scope is extraterritorial — if your AI system is placed on the EU market or its output is used in the EU, you are in scope regardless of where the model is trained or hosted. Penalties scale from €7.5M up to €35M or 7% of global annual turnover.

Why CISMs Need to Care

Prohibited practices took effect February 2025. GPAI model obligations took effect August 2025. High-risk AI system obligations take effect August 2, 2026. Article 73 serious-incident reporting also begins August 2026. The Act is in force; the question is no longer "if" but "where do our systems land?"

The Four Risk Tiers

The Act classifies AI systems into four risk tiers. Obligations scale with risk. Classification is the cheapest decision in the entire compliance program and the one teams most often get wrong.

🚫
Unacceptable Risk
Prohibited entirely. Social scoring by governments, real-time biometric ID in public spaces (with narrow exceptions), emotion recognition in workplaces/schools, subliminal manipulation, exploitation of vulnerabilities.
BANNED
⚠️
High Risk
Permitted with strict conformity requirements. AI in critical infrastructure, education access, employment decisions, essential services (credit scoring), law enforcement, border control, justice administration — see Annex III.
FULL CONFORMITY
ℹ️
Limited Risk
Permitted with transparency obligations. Chatbots interacting with humans, AI-generated content (deepfakes, synthetic media), emotion recognition outside prohibited contexts, biometric categorization.
TRANSPARENCY
✅
Minimal Risk
Permitted with no specific Act obligations. The vast majority of current AI applications — spam filters, AI-enabled video games, basic recommendation engines.
NO SPECIFIC RULES

Interactive Risk-Tier Classifier

Walk a real AI system through the EU AI Act's classification logic. Answer the questions in order — the tool will land you in the correct tier.

1Prohibited Practice Check

Does the system perform any of these: social scoring by public authorities; real-time remote biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces (outside narrow law-enforcement exceptions); emotion recognition in workplaces or educational institutions; subliminal manipulation; exploitation of vulnerabilities of specific groups?

2Safety Component Check

Is the AI system a safety component of a product (or itself a product) already covered by EU harmonized product safety legislation listed in Annex I — e.g., medical devices, machinery, toys, lifts, vehicles, aviation?

3Annex III Check

Does the system fall into any Annex III category: biometric identification/categorization; critical infrastructure (water, gas, electricity); education and vocational training (admissions, evaluation); employment, worker management, access to self-employment; access to essential services (credit scoring, public benefits, insurance); law enforcement; migration, asylum, border control; administration of justice and democratic processes?

4Limited Risk Check

Does the system (a) interact directly with natural persons (chatbot, virtual assistant); (b) generate or manipulate synthetic content (deepfakes, AI-generated text/images/audio/video); (c) detect emotions outside prohibited contexts; or (d) perform biometric categorization?

5GPAI Check

Is this a general-purpose AI model — trained on broad data at scale, capable of serving many different downstream tasks (e.g., a large language model offered as an API or product)?

🚫 UNACCEPTABLE RISK — PROHIBITED

This system is banned under the EU AI Act. It cannot be placed on the EU market, put into service in the EU, or used in the EU. Prohibition penalties: up to €35M or 7% of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.

⚠️ HIGH RISK — Full Conformity Required

This system requires: risk management system, data governance, technical documentation, record-keeping, transparency to deployers, human oversight, accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity, conformity assessment before market placement, EU declaration of conformity, CE marking, post-market monitoring, and serious-incident reporting (Article 73, effective August 2026). Penalties for non-compliance: up to €15M or 3% of global annual turnover.

ℹ️ LIMITED RISK — Transparency Obligations

Article 50 transparency obligations apply: users must be informed they are interacting with an AI (unless obvious); AI-generated content must be marked as artificially generated/manipulated; emotion recognition and biometric categorization must be disclosed to affected persons. Effective August 2026.

✅ MINIMAL RISK — No Specific Act Obligations

The Act imposes no specific obligations beyond existing law (GDPR, product safety, etc.). Voluntary codes of conduct encouraged. Document the classification reasoning in a signed memo and refresh at least quarterly — the system might be re-categorized as deployment evolves.

📦 GENERAL-PURPOSE AI MODEL — Title V Obligations

GPAI model obligations apply: technical documentation, downstream provider documentation, copyright policy, training data summary. Additional obligations apply to GPAI models with "systemic risk" (highest-capability models). Note: a GPAI model integrated into a downstream system still needs separate classification of that system.

Provider vs. Deployer — A New Distinction with Teeth

The EU AI Act splits responsibility between the entity that builds the AI system and the entity that uses it. Most CISMs work for organizations that are deployers, not providers — but the line is not always obvious.

RoleDefinitionPrimary Obligations
ProviderDevelops an AI system or has one developed and places it on the EU market under its own name/trademarkThe bulk of high-risk obligations: design, conformity assessment, technical documentation, CE marking, post-market monitoring, incident reporting
Deployer (formerly "user")Uses an AI system under its authority (not for personal non-professional use)Use according to provider instructions, human oversight, input data relevance, log retention, FRIA for certain high-risk uses, transparency to affected persons
ImporterPlaces on the EU market an AI system bearing the name/trademark of a non-EU entityVerify provider has performed conformity assessment, technical documentation is available, system carries CE marking
DistributorMakes the system available on the EU market without affecting its propertiesVerify CE marking, accompanying documentation, provider/importer details
The Trap

A deployer that substantially modifies a high-risk AI system, or puts it into service under their own name, can be reclassified as a provider — with all the heavier obligations that implies. Fine-tuning a foundation model for a high-risk use case is the canonical example. "We're just deploying it" can quietly become "we're now the provider" depending on what you do to the model.

Module 6 60 min

AI Risk Taxonomy — What You're Actually Managing

A CISM is trained to manage information security risk: confidentiality, integrity, availability, with some adjacency to privacy and operational risk. AI introduces categories that don't fit cleanly into those buckets. Mature AI risk programs use an expanded taxonomy.

The Seven Risk Categories

🧠

Model Risk

The model itself behaves incorrectly — hallucinates, drifts, fails on edge cases, performs worse on subpopulations than on average.

Example: A medical triage model that performs 15% worse on female patients than male patients because the training data was male-skewed.
📊

Data Risk

The data used to train, fine-tune, or query the model creates problems — poisoned, biased, leaking, copyrighted, regulated, or inappropriate.

Example: Customer PII inadvertently included in fine-tuning data, then memorized and reproducible in outputs.
⚔️

Adversarial Risk

Attackers actively target the system — prompt injection, model evasion, model extraction, model inversion, training data poisoning.

Example: An indirect prompt injection hidden in a document fed to an AI assistant exfiltrates the user's emails.
🔗

Third-Party / Supply Chain Risk

The AI system depends on models, data, or services your organization doesn't control — and you inherit their vulnerabilities, biases, and outages.

Example: A foundation model provider changes safety filtering rules; your customer-facing chatbot starts producing outputs that violate your brand standards.
⚖️

Ethical & Societal Risk

The system causes harm even when functioning as designed — discrimination, displacement, deception, dignitary harm.

Example: A resume screening model trained on historical hiring data systematically downgrades qualified candidates from groups that were historically underrepresented.
📜

Regulatory & Legal Risk

The deployment violates a binding rule — EU AI Act, sector regulation, IP law, data protection law, consumer protection law.

Example: Using a high-risk AI system in employment decisions without the required conformity assessment, FRIA, and human oversight.
🏢

Operational & Business Risk

The AI fails to deliver value, creates dependency on a single provider, blows up costs unexpectedly, or damages reputation through visible failure.

Example: A customer support chatbot confidently invents a refund policy that the company has to honor when it goes viral.
Analogy: The Pharmaceutical Risk Framework

A pharmaceutical company doesn't just ask "does the drug work?" It asks: efficacy (does it do what it claims), safety (does it harm), quality (is it manufactured consistently), supply (can we source the ingredients), regulatory (is it approved), and commercial (will it sell). AI risk works the same way — looking at only one or two dimensions gives a falsely reassuring picture.

Risk Likelihood and Impact — The AI Wrinkle

A CISM is comfortable with likelihood-times-impact risk math. AI introduces a complication: some AI risks have heavy-tailed distributions. The expected behavior is fine 99% of the time; the 1% failure is catastrophic.

  • Hallucination in a customer service chatbot — mostly harmless; occasionally produces a binding commitment the company has to honor.
  • Bias in a hiring screen — invisible in aggregate; surfaces as a class-action lawsuit.
  • Prompt injection on an agentic system — almost never triggered by users; devastating when triggered by an attacker.

This means traditional sampling-based testing is necessary but not sufficient. AAISM-level practice includes adversarial testing (red-teaming), edge-case probing, and continuous monitoring of subpopulation performance.

Practice: Categorizing AI Risks

A loan approval model's accuracy has dropped 8% over six months as economic conditions have shifted from the training period.
Employees have been pasting confidential customer records into a public chatbot to get summaries.
Researchers demonstrate that hidden instructions in PDF documents can make your AI assistant exfiltrate emails to an attacker-controlled URL.
The foundation model your product is built on has its API pricing increased by 4x with 60 days' notice.
An audit reveals that an AI-driven promotion-recommendation tool consistently scores employees with longer career gaps lower, disproportionately affecting women who took parental leave.
Your company deployed an AI-powered CV ranking system in the EU without a conformity assessment, technical documentation, or registered fundamental rights impact assessment.
Module 7 60 min

Shadow AI — The Governance Iceberg

"Shadow AI" is the AI equivalent of shadow IT: AI tools and capabilities being used inside the enterprise without the knowledge, approval, or oversight of IT, security, or governance functions. It is not a hypothetical. It is the default state of most enterprises in 2026.

Why Shadow AI Is Different from Shadow IT

DimensionShadow ITShadow AI
Adoption frictionRequires installing software, often a credit cardFree, web-based, no installation, often inside existing tools
Detection difficultyNetwork monitoring can catch most unauthorized SaaSOften indistinguishable from normal web traffic; embedded in approved tools
Data exfiltration vectorFile uploads to unauthorized cloud storagePasting sensitive data into prompts — far easier, far less visible
Output ownershipMostly clear (you made it, you own it)Murky — IP status of AI-generated content varies by jurisdiction
Governance baselineGenerally treated as a security issueOften treated as a productivity tool; governance hasn't caught up
The Iceberg

When CISOs survey their organizations, they typically find that between 3x and 10x more AI tools are in active use than IT is aware of. The visible AI deployments are the iceberg's tip. The mass below the waterline is shadow AI — and it is doing real work, with real data, every day.

The Five Shadow AI Patterns

🌐

Direct Consumer Use

Employees use public chatbots (ChatGPT, Claude.ai, Gemini, etc.) on their work tasks, often via personal accounts, often with sensitive data.

🔌

Embedded Feature Use

AI features arrive inside already-approved tools (CRM, IDE, office suite) — adopted without anyone realizing a new AI dependency now exists.

🌉

Bridge Tools

Browser extensions, plugins, and integration apps that route enterprise data through external AI services as a side effect.

🤖

Citizen Developer Agents

Business users build automation, chatbots, and agents using low-code tools without security or architecture review.

📱

Personal Productivity Tools

Note-taking, meeting transcription, and writing-assistant apps that quietly upload work content to external AI services.

Practice: Shadow AI Scenarios

Scenario 1 — The Meeting Transcriber
Medium Risk
A salesperson installed a popular AI meeting-transcription app on their laptop. It joins Zoom calls automatically and emails them summaries. They've been using it on client calls for six weeks. IT discovers it during a routine endpoint audit.
Scenario 2 — The CRM Surprise
High Risk
Your CRM vendor pushes an update that adds an "AI Assistant" sidebar to every record. It summarizes customer notes, drafts follow-up emails, and suggests next actions. The feature is enabled by default. Your security team finds out from a LinkedIn post by the vendor's CEO.
Scenario 3 — The Marketing Copilot
Medium Risk
The marketing team has been using a popular AI image-generation service to produce campaign visuals. The contracts and licensing terms have never been reviewed. Some of the images closely resemble copyrighted characters and styles. The team is about to launch a campaign featuring these images.
Scenario 4 — The Code Assistant
Critical Risk
A developer has been using a public code-completion AI tool in their IDE to write code for an internal financial reporting system. The tool sends code context to an external service to generate suggestions. The reporting system handles regulated financial data; the developer pasted database schemas and sample records into prompts to get better suggestions.
The Governance Pattern

Notice the pattern across the scenarios: understand scope, assess legitimate need, evaluate approved alternatives, then govern. Bans without alternatives fail. Punishment without policy fails. The right response is almost always to formalize the use case, not eliminate it.

AI Vendor & Supply Chain Risk Management

The AAISM exam outline calls out a specific domain area — "AI Vendor and Supply Chain Management" — because the AI supply chain is unlike any traditional IT supply chain. An AI system is rarely something you fully build or fully buy; it is assembled from layered dependencies, most of which you do not control.

The Five-Layer AI Supply Chain

LayerWhat's ThereWho Typically Owns ItKey Risk
1. Foundation modelThe pretrained large model (LLM, vision, multimodal)Major AI lab (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, etc.)Provider changes safety policy, pricing, or terms; model deprecated; provider outage
2. Training dataPretraining corpus + fine-tuning datasetsFoundation model provider (opaque); fine-tunerCopyright, regulated data, poisoning, bias
3. Fine-tuning / RAG layerCustomizations: fine-tunes, prompts, retrieval data, embeddingsYour organization, your vendors, your contractorsSensitive data leakage; prompt injection through retrieval; embedding leakage
4. Application layerThe product or service wrapping the modelSaaS vendor or your dev teamImproper output handling, excessive agency, insecure plugin chains
5. Tools / integrationsExternal tools the AI can call (databases, APIs, file systems, code execution)Mix of internal and third-partyBlast radius of compromised prompt — agent can act on your behalf
Analogy: Food Supply Chain

You are a restaurant. The foundation model is the wheat. The fine-tuning data is your secret recipe. The application is your kitchen. The tools are your suppliers, ovens, and delivery drivers. A contamination, a recipe leak, a supplier failure, or an oven malfunction can each ruin the meal — and you might be liable even if the wheat is what was contaminated. AI supply chain due diligence is the equivalent of food safety inspection across the whole chain.

Buy vs. Build vs. Consume

AAISM exam guidance distinguishes "Consumer vs. Enterprise" and "Buy vs. Build" as foundational AI procurement decisions. A CISM should be able to articulate the security tradeoffs of each.

🏗️ Build (or heavily customize)
  • Maximum control over data, behavior, deployment
  • Heaviest regulatory burden — you are the provider
  • Hardest to keep up with state of the art
  • Best for: highly differentiated use cases, regulated industries with strict data sovereignty
🛒 Buy / Consume (SaaS / API)
  • Fastest time to value, latest capabilities
  • You inherit vendor's choices on safety, data handling, defaults
  • Often deployer role under EU AI Act — lighter regulatory burden
  • Best for: common use cases where parity with the market is enough

What to Demand of an AI Vendor — A Procurement Checklist

  • What happens to inputs (prompts, uploaded documents) — retained? for how long? used for training?
  • Can we opt out of training on our inputs? Is opt-out the default?
  • Where is data processed? Stored? Backed up? Any cross-border transfers?
  • What's the deletion process? How is it verified?
  • Sub-processors — who else gets to see our data?
  • Which foundation model is in use? Is the choice documented and version-pinned?
  • Model card — what does it disclose about training data, evaluation, limitations?
  • What happens when the underlying model is updated or deprecated?
  • Can we test the model independently? Are evals available?
  • Disclosure of known biases or failure modes for our use case?
  • Third-party attestations: SOC 2, ISO 27001 — and now ISO/IEC 42001 for AIMS.
  • Penetration testing cadence; AI-specific red-teaming results?
  • Vulnerability disclosure program and AI-specific reporting channel?
  • Incident notification SLA — including AI-specific incidents (prompt injection, data leakage, model misuse)?
  • Access controls, encryption in transit and at rest, key management?
  • IP indemnification — vendor indemnifies you against claims that AI outputs infringe third-party IP.
  • Data Processing Agreement (DPA) under GDPR / similar.
  • Service Level Agreement (SLA) — uptime, latency, support response.
  • Liability cap — is it reasonable given the use case? Are there carve-outs for AI-specific incidents?
  • Audit rights — can you (or a third party) audit AI-specific controls?
  • Exit and data portability — what do you get back if you leave?
  • Published responsible AI principles — and evidence they're operationalized.
  • Bias testing for your population of interest — protected classes, languages, contexts.
  • Human-in-the-loop and override mechanisms appropriate to risk level.
  • EU AI Act readiness — provider obligations met if high-risk? Documentation available?
  • Sustainability disclosure — energy and water consumption per query/training run?
The Procurement Trap

Existing vendor management processes were not designed to catch AI-specific risks. An AI vendor questionnaire is a distinct artifact from your standard SaaS questionnaire — it overlaps maybe 60%. If procurement is running AI vendors through the old questionnaire, you are missing the questions that matter most.

Module 9 45 min

Building an AI Acceptable Use Policy That Holds Up

An AI Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) is the single most-asked-for document in any AI governance program, and the single most often produced badly. Most first drafts are too vague to be actionable and too rigid to be realistic. A good AI AUP is specific, scenario-driven, and lives alongside an approval pathway — not in place of one.

The Seven Required Sections of an AI AUP

State what the policy applies to. Be specific. Naming "AI" is not enough — distinguish generative AI, predictive ML, decision-support systems, AI assistants embedded in approved tools, and AI-powered third-party services. State the audience (employees, contractors, vendors) and geographic scope.

Anti-pattern: "This policy covers all uses of artificial intelligence at the company."

Better: "This policy covers (a) any use of generative AI systems by employees or contractors, including via web interfaces, APIs, plugins, or embedded features; (b) any AI system deployed by the company to make or support decisions affecting customers, employees, or third parties; (c) any AI-related procurement decision over $10,000 or affecting regulated data."

For each data classification level, state which AI systems may be used. This is the section employees actually need — most violations come from people who didn't know the rule, not people who knew and ignored it.

Data ClassSanctioned Enterprise AIPublic AI Tools
Public✓ Allowed✓ Allowed
Internal✓ Allowed✗ Prohibited
Confidential✓ Allowed with approval✗ Prohibited
Restricted / Regulated✗ Prohibited without explicit DPO + Legal approval✗ Prohibited

Be explicit. Prohibited use cases typically include: making consequential decisions about people (hiring, firing, promotion, credit, healthcare) without human review and documented oversight; impersonating real people; generating deceptive content; circumventing access controls or safety measures; producing content that violates law or company policy; using AI to write code for security-sensitive systems without review.

Plain-language test: An employee should be able to read this section and know, within 30 seconds, whether what they're about to do is allowed.

For every AI output that drives an action with non-trivial consequences, state who reviews and who is accountable. Patterns: human-in-the-loop (every output reviewed before action), human-on-the-loop (humans supervise but don't review each output), human-out-of-the-loop (fully automated, with monitoring).

The required pattern should scale with risk: HITL for hiring decisions, HOTL for content moderation queue triage, HOOL for routine email sorting.

A policy without an approval pathway is a policy without compliance. Document who employees go to when they want to use an AI tool the policy doesn't cover. State the expected response time. Use a tiered model — light-touch self-registration for low-risk uses, full review for high-risk uses.

Pattern: The fastest way to kill shadow AI is to make sanctioned AI faster and easier to adopt than shadow AI.

State when AI use must be disclosed: to customers (when interacting with a bot, when receiving AI-generated content), to colleagues (when work product is substantially AI-generated), to leadership (when AI is used in consequential decisions). The EU AI Act's Article 50 sets a regulatory floor; many organizations should commit to more.

State that the policy is enforceable and that violations have consequences proportionate to severity. Tie to existing employee conduct policies. Note that good-faith reports of shadow AI by the user themselves should not result in punishment — you want disclosure, not concealment.

The "Lives Alongside" Principle

The biggest single failure of AI AUPs is treating the policy as the governance program. A policy without an approval pathway, a use case register, a vendor questionnaire, and an enforcement mechanism is theater. The AUP is one artifact in a multi-artifact program.

AI Governance Build — TerraHealth Insurance

Scenario

You are the CISM at TerraHealth, a $2.1B regional health insurance company with operations in the US (NY, NJ, PA, CT) and a small EU subsidiary based in Dublin. The board has asked the CISO to "develop a comprehensive AI governance program within 90 days" after the following events:

  • Customer service team has been using a popular consumer chatbot to draft member-facing letters for the past four months. A draft response containing PHI was inadvertently sent without review; the member's diagnosis was inaccurately summarized.
  • The claims team deployed an internal "auto-denial" model six months ago without governance review. A state insurance regulator has opened an inquiry after a local newspaper investigated unusual denial patterns.
  • The CFO wants to deploy a vendor-provided "AI-powered fraud detection" system that classifies claims as fraudulent for human review. The vendor is in the EU.
  • The CEO is excited about using an AI tool to "personalize wellness recommendations" for members — and has been talking to two startup vendors directly, bypassing procurement.
  • The Dublin subsidiary processes claims from EU members. Several AI-powered tools are already in use locally.

Your Required Deliverables

  1. AI governance structure — committee composition, charter, cadence, decision authority, escalation path
  2. AI use case register — initial entries for the four use cases above, with classification, risk tier (including EU AI Act tier where applicable), and owner
  3. Risk assessment — for each of the four scenarios, identify the top three risks from the risk taxonomy and propose treatment
  4. Regulatory mapping — which obligations apply (EU AI Act, HIPAA, state insurance regulation, NYDFS Part 500, etc.) and to which systems
  5. AI acceptable use policy — covering data classification rules for AI inputs, prohibited use cases, approval pathway, and disclosure requirements
  6. Vendor risk assessment — for the fraud detection vendor and one of the wellness startups, run the procurement checklist and produce a recommendation
  7. 30/60/90-day roadmap — what gets done in the first month, the second, the third — and what executive decisions are required at each phase
